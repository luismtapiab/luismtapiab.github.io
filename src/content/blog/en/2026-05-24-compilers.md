My love for compilers didnt come from the first course on it, I almost failed the one on parsers until i forced myself to complete the book exercises we had  and i aced my final. then the next course I had a great teacher and a much more eagerness to learn and I ended up really enjoying it and even building a compiler for my thesis. The tought of builidng a compiler for a programming language was mind blowing to in the first years we used to look to people who made something like that as crazy but i turn out to be one of them, for the good.

The course was Compiler Design and our teacher was Pablo Azero. the main project was to implement a complier for a subset of Java to SSM2 a simple virtual machine that had instructions like push, pop, add, sub, mult, div, call, ret, etc.

First we were doing each one a parser for a given grammar, some subset of java and then we exchange them to reach a good solution but everyone kept working on its one until the end. I remember seeing how my classmates did and also how some parts of my solution were used in the core of my classmates parsers. We were reading outside of class and every week we come with the homework and we start reviewing the problems everyone had and sharing our approaches. I remember it didnt feel like the teacher was testing us but helping us build a better solution, that made me enjoy the process a lot. We made pair programming with him too, maybe debugging is a little more appropiate name for it.
When i tought of one teaching method that makes you enjoy the process of learning i remembered the course on compilers. There were few people in the end but those who stayed learned the most from it. I have pending to make a course equally compelling for my students.

I learned a lot about name analysis, semantic analysis, code generation, error handling and more, but even more important I learned how to think about the product of a program in terms of what it does, not how it is written. 

In my thesis i learnt about new techniques on building a compiler from a brief specification of the language. Also it was a great experience using an undisclosed project at the time, reaching out to some authors and learning from them directly. some times i was lost but i put all my effort to understand the solution and make it work. i talked before about this on a previous post [CBS](/blog/en/2026-05-20-cbs/).

Building a compiler is a great way to learn how programming languages work, it teaches you how to think about the product of a program in terms of what it does, not how it is written.
